# HubSpot Partner Billing Research: Can Vernayo Bill for a Competing CRM SaaS?

**Research Date:** 2026-05-07
**Subject:** Léo Jaqueline / Vernayo (SIREN 932 848 815) — HubSpot partner status and permissibility of billing clients for a managed Twenty CRM SaaS service
**Prepared for:** GB Consult / leCRM project — internal use only

---

## Executive Verdict

**The short answer is: probably yes, with caveats that depend heavily on Leo's actual partner tier.**

The HubSpot Solutions Partner Program Agreement (SPPA) is explicitly **non-exclusive** (Section 2). It contains no blanket prohibition on partners recommending, implementing, or billing for competing CRM products. However, three risk vectors exist: (1) the catch-all "negatively reflect" termination clause (Section 12.d), (2) the requirement that partners not engage in "deceptive, misleading, or unethical" activities (Section 17.f), and (3) possible tier-specific conduct expectations not fully codified in the public agreement.

**Structure A (apporteur d'affaires) carries the lowest SPPA risk.** Leo's name does not appear on client invoices; his role is purely referral. Structure B (Vernayo as prime contractor) carries moderate risk if Vernayo is actively marketed as a HubSpot partner while simultaneously billing clients for a direct HubSpot competitor. Structure C is an intermediate position.

**Critical prerequisite:** The SPPA only binds organisations formally enrolled in the Solutions Partner Program. If Leo holds only individual HubSpot Academy certifications and has never formally accepted the SPPA as a registered entity, the agreement may not apply to him at all. This must be verified before applying these findings.

## 1. SPPA Clause Analysis

### Source accessibility

The Solutions Partner Program Agreement is publicly accessible at [https://legal.hubspot.com/solutions-partner-program-agreement](https://legal.hubspot.com/solutions-partner-program-agreement) and was last substantively updated on November 17, 2025, with prior updates on June 2, 2025, and October 1, 2025. The analysis below is based on the current (May 2026) public text.

### Section 2 — Non-Exclusivity (the key clause)

> "This Agreement does not create an exclusive agreement between you and us. Both you and we will have the right to recommend similar products and services of third parties and to work with other parties in connection with the design, sale, installation, implementation and use of similar services and products of third parties."

This is the most important clause for the present question. It explicitly permits HubSpot partners to recommend and work with competing CRM products. There is no list of prohibited competitors, no geographic carve-out, and no exception for direct competitors. The non-exclusivity clause is mutual: HubSpot can also work with other partners and recommend third-party products.

**Implication:** Neither recommending Twenty CRM nor implementing it for a client is expressly prohibited by the SPPA. The clause language is broad enough to cover a managed SaaS billing model.

### Section 3.b — Program Compliance

> "You will comply, and your relevant employees, representatives, consultants, contractors or agents comply, with the terms and conditions of this Agreement at all times, including the Program Policies."

Partners are required to comply with Program Policies (a separate document at [https://www.hubspot.com/partners/solutions-program-policies](https://www.hubspot.com/partners/solutions-program-policies)), which do not currently contain explicit competing-product restrictions. However, this clause means any future policy update is immediately binding.

### Section 12.d — Termination for Cause

HubSpot may terminate the agreement immediately if a partner is:

> "Acting in a way that has or may negatively reflect on or affect us"

This is the principal risk clause. It is deliberately vague and gives HubSpot broad discretion. Importantly, enforcement intelligence gathered from community sources indicates this clause is applied primarily to reputational misconduct (e.g., publicly disparaging HubSpot to clients, deceptive co-marketing), **not** to the mere existence of a competing product offering. A search of the HubSpot Community legal update threads (November 2025, November 3, 2025, September 2024, March 2024, January 2024) found no mention of partners terminated under this clause for selling or billing a competing CRM.

The Technology Partner Program Agreement contains comparable language but with an explicit carve-out: "this clause shall not apply to normal competitive activities that a partner may engage in." The Solutions Partner version does not contain this carve-out verbatim, which is worth noting — though the non-exclusivity clause in Section 2 effectively creates an analogous protection for service partners.

### Section 17.f — Ethical Marketing

> "You shall not engage in any deceptive, misleading, illegal or unethical marketing activities, or activities that otherwise may be detrimental to us, our customers, or to the public."

This clause is relevant if Vernayo's marketing explicitly uses its HubSpot partner badge to sell a product that directly undermines a client's HubSpot investment. For example, if Leo presents himself as a "HubSpot partner" during a pitch specifically designed to move a prospect away from HubSpot and toward leCRM, this could be characterised as using the HubSpot badge deceptively.

### Section 7.c — Optional Program Commercialisation Restrictions

> "Partners may not commercialize Optional Programs such as optional tools or benefits. You are prohibited from selling or reselling such Optional Programs or any specific use thereof."

This clause applies only to HubSpot-provided tools and benefits given to partners (e.g., demo portals, NFR licences). It does not restrict what other products partners can sell. It is not directly relevant to the leCRM product.

### Section 4.a — Revenue Share Eligibility

Partners cannot earn HubSpot revenue share on transactions involving competing products — that restriction is inherent to the revenue share structure itself (it only pays on HubSpot-sold subscriptions). There is no clause prohibiting parallel revenue from non-HubSpot products.

### Structure A Assessment

Leo earns a referral fee from GB Consult. He is not on the client invoice. He does not negotiate the contract. This is the cleanest position under the SPPA. The only residual risk is if Leo uses his HubSpot partner identity during the sales pitch to clients who ultimately buy leCRM instead of HubSpot — which could be characterised as a Section 17.f misuse of the HubSpot brand. Mitigation: Leo should present leCRM opportunities in a personal capacity, without deploying HubSpot partner credentials.

### Structure B Assessment

Vernayo invoices the client for a managed CRM service that is a direct HubSpot competitor. If Vernayo's public profile simultaneously emphasises HubSpot partner status, this creates a visible tension. However, the SPPA Section 2 non-exclusivity clause means no explicit prohibition is breached. The risk concentrates in Section 12.d and Section 17.f. Enforcement likelihood is low absent overt disparagement of HubSpot or a direct complaint from a HubSpot sales rep who lost a deal.

**Key mitigation:** Operate leCRM under a distinct brand (e.g., a separate GB Consult sub-brand or a distinct Vernayo service line) that does not prominently display the HubSpot partner badge. This is the single most effective risk reduction measure for Structure B.

### Structure C Assessment

Setup invoiced by Vernayo (consulting/integration framing), MRR invoiced by GB Consult. The setup fee is arguably pure consulting, which is clearly within any partner's rights. The structural separation of MRR billing to GB Consult reduces Leo's direct billing exposure for the competing SaaS element. This is a reasonable middle ground if tax efficiency and SPPA caution are both priorities.

## 4. Practical Enforcement Reality

### Has HubSpot ever terminated a partner for competing-product activity?

No public documented case of SPPA termination specifically for implementing or billing a competing CRM was found in a search of news sources, MarTech industry press, Reddit (r/hubspot), HubSpot Community legal threads, or LinkedIn discussions as of May 2026.

What was found: A MarTech.org article on the 2025 HubSpot/Salesforce partner program restructuring noted that partner programs are tightening activity requirements (minimum sourced deals, platform investment) — but the enforcement focus is on performance, not competitive exclusivity. Partners who are not generating HubSpot revenue may be downtiered or exited, but not for selling competing products. Source: [Salesforce and HubSpot reshape partner programs for the AI era — MarTech](https://martech.org/salesforce-and-hubspot-reshape-partner-programs-for-the-ai-era/)

### Multi-CRM partners in practice

Multiple HubSpot Diamond and Platinum partners openly advertise migration services from Salesforce, Pipedrive, Zoho, and Monday.com to HubSpot — and also implement those same competing platforms. Examples: Market Veep (HubSpot Diamond partner) explicitly lists Salesforce, Pipedrive, and Zoho connectivity services. ATAK Interactive (Platinum) handles full CRM migrations from Salesforce, Pipedrive, and Zoho. Neither firm has reported termination or warning for this cross-platform practice.

No partner was found in a search of ecosystem.hubspot.com or public directories who openly bills clients for a managed competing CRM SaaS as a primary product line while retaining HubSpot partner status. This gap exists not because it is prohibited, but because most HubSpot partners have no business reason to do it. The leCRM scenario is atypical in that GB Consult's product is targeted at the same SMB segment HubSpot targets, pitched specifically to prospects who rejected HubSpot. This is the nuance that elevates risk beyond the typical "we also do Salesforce migrations" pattern.

### HubSpot's general posture

HubSpot's enforcement posture is commercially pragmatic. The company earns no revenue from terminating partners and loses goodwill. Informal community intelligence and the structure of the SPPA itself suggest HubSpot is unlikely to proactively monitor whether a partner is building or billing a competing open-source CRM, particularly at the micro-entrepreneur scale of Vernayo. The more realistic risk is a HubSpot account executive who loses a deal to leCRM and flags the partner relationship — which would trigger a review rather than automatic termination.

## 5. Leo's Actual Partner Status — Verification Path

This is the most important open question before applying the findings above.

### The critical distinction

Being HubSpot-certified (individual HubSpot Academy credentials) does **not** make a person bound by the SPPA. The SPPA binds an organisation that has formally enrolled in the Solutions Partner Program (Provider or Partner tier). A person who has completed HubSpot certifications independently and describes themselves as a "HubSpot partner" may be doing so informally, without any binding agreement with HubSpot.

If Vernayo has never formally enrolled as a Solutions Provider or Solutions Partner, the SPPA does not apply. Leo can bill for any CRM service he likes.

### How Leo can verify his actual status

1. **Partner Portal:** Log into [https://app.hubspot.com/](https://app.hubspot.com/) with the Vernayo account and check whether there is a "Partner" section or partner tier badge displayed in the account dashboard.
2. **SPPA acceptance:** Partners must affirmatively accept the SPPA during enrolment. If Leo has never accepted it, he is not bound. Acceptance is recorded in the HubSpot partner portal under legal agreements.
3. **Partner Directory check:** Search [https://ecosystem.hubspot.com/marketplace/solutions](https://ecosystem.hubspot.com/marketplace/solutions) for "Vernayo" or "Léo Jacqueline." A public directory listing confirms formal Solutions Partner status. As of May 2026, no listing for Vernayo was found in this search — which suggests Leo may be certified individually but not formally enrolled as a Solutions Partner entity.
4. **Partner Development Manager:** If Leo has a dedicated PDM assigned by HubSpot, he is a formal partner. If not, he is likely operating only with individual certifications.

### Preliminary finding

The absence of a Vernayo listing in the HubSpot Solutions Marketplace directory, combined with Leo's micro-entrepreneur structure (which is not typically the entity type enrolled as a formal Solutions Partner — those are usually SARLs, SASUs, or larger agencies), suggests there is a material possibility that Leo holds individual certifications only and is not formally bound by the SPPA. This should be confirmed before the team invests further analysis in SPPA compliance.

## 6. Recommended Structure

**Primary recommendation: Structure A with planned migration to Structure C.**

For immediate launches, Structure A (apporteur d'affaires) is cleanest. Leo introduces clients, GB Consult bills, Leo receives a referral fee from GB Consult. Leo's HubSpot partner identity remains strictly in the HubSpot context; it is never deployed in leCRM client-facing materials.

As revenue grows and the tax efficiency of Structure B or C becomes significant, transition to Structure C: Vernayo invoices a one-time onboarding/integration consulting fee (clearly scoped as technical setup, not subscription), and GB Consult invoices the MRR. This limits Vernayo's billing exposure to a consulting framing that is unambiguously within any partner's rights.

Structure B (Vernayo prime, including MRR) is viable if Leo first confirms that Vernayo is not listed in the Solutions Partner Directory as a formal partner entity — i.e., he operates only under individual certifications. In that case, the SPPA does not bind Vernayo, and there is no legal obstacle at all.

## 7. Mitigation Playbook (if Structure B is Chosen for Tax Reasons)

If tax optimisation requires Vernayo to bill the full service, implement the following mitigations in order of priority:

**Mitigation 1 — Brand separation (highest impact)**
Do not use the "HubSpot Solutions Partner" badge, co-marketing materials, or HubSpot-associated visual identity in any leCRM client-facing materials. Operate leCRM under a separate product name (already planned: "leCRM" is distinct from HubSpot). Ensure the Vernayo website's leCRM service description makes no reference to HubSpot partner status.

**Mitigation 2 — Scope demarcation in contracts**
Vernayo's engagement letters with leCRM clients should clearly describe the service as "hosted CRM software based on Twenty (open-source, AGPL-3.0)" and make no comparison to or implied endorsement by HubSpot. Include a clause that Vernayo's HubSpot certifications are held in a separate professional capacity.

**Mitigation 3 — No cross-promotion**
Do not use the leCRM client list as a HubSpot prospecting list, and do not use HubSpot partner referrals to source leCRM prospects. This avoids any argument that HubSpot-sourced deal information was repurposed for competitive benefit (Section 4.c).

**Mitigation 4 — Proactive disclosure to HubSpot (optional but beneficial)**
Leo may choose to notify his Partner Development Manager (if he has one) of his involvement in a separate managed CRM offering. This establishes good faith and eliminates the possibility of a "you should have told us" argument in a later dispute. Most partners in analogous situations do not disclose proactively; the risk of not disclosing is low given HubSpot's enforcement posture. This is recommended only if Leo has a close PDM relationship and is confident the PDM will not flag it internally as a commercial threat.

**Mitigation 5 — Entity separation (longer-term)**
If the leCRM business scales significantly, consider incorporating it under a separate legal entity (e.g., a distinct SASU co-owned by Leo and Guillaume). This creates clean separation between Vernayo (HubSpot partner identity) and the competing CRM entity. Under French law, two separate legal entities are entirely distinct even if controlled by the same person, provided governance, accounting, and client-facing identity are kept separate.

## 8. Comparable Industry Patterns

HubSpot agencies that also work with competing platforms typically fall into two categories:

**Migration specialists:** Agencies certified in HubSpot that also handle Salesforce, Zoho, and Pipedrive migrations. These firms retain HubSpot partner status because migration toward HubSpot is the primary use case. They are not billing clients for ongoing competing CRM subscriptions.

**Multi-platform consultancies:** Rare among formal HubSpot Solutions Partners. Most "multi-CRM" consultants are not formally enrolled in the HubSpot Solutions Partner Program; they hold individual certifications only. This confirms that the most practically low-risk posture for the leCRM project is for Leo to clarify (and if necessary exit) his formal Solutions Partner enrollment, while retaining individual HubSpot certifications which carry no SPPA obligations.

**Open-source CRM integrators:** No documented case was found of a formal HubSpot Solutions Partner who simultaneously operates a managed SaaS service on Mautic, EspoCRM, or Twenty. The category is genuinely novel at the formal-partner level. This novelty is itself a risk signal: HubSpot has no established precedent for how to handle it, which means outcomes are unpredictable if the situation ever comes to their attention.
